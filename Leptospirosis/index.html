What is Leptospirosis ?

Leptospirosis is a bacterial infection caused by the Leptospira species, primarily transmitted to humans through contact with water or soil contaminated by the urine of infected animals, such as rodents. This zoonotic disease can lead to a range of symptoms, from mild flu-like signs to severe complications, including kidney failure and liver damage. The infection is particularly prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions, where environmental conditions favor the survival of the bacteria. Awareness and preventive measures, such as avoiding contaminated water and practicing good hygiene, are crucial in reducing the risk of leptospirosis, especially for those engaged in outdoor activities or working in agriculture.

Local Leptospirosis Dashboard

Leptospirosis is a significant public health concern in Sri Lanka, characterized by its endemic presence across the country. This interactive dashboard provides a comprehensive exploration of local leptospirosis cases from 2007 to the present, offering valuable insights and visualizations that highlight trends and patterns in the data.
By analyzing the data presented in the dashboard, users can identify high-risk periods, such as the monsoon seasons when cases typically surge, and understand the geographical distribution of cases across different districts. The dashboard not only visualizes the incidence rates but also correlates them with environmental factors, agricultural practices, and demographic data, allowing for a multifaceted view of the disease's impact on communities.
Moreover, the dashboard provides a platform for public health officials, researchers, and the general public to engage with the data actively. It highlights the importance of early detection and intervention strategies, as well as the need for ongoing education about preventive measures, especially for those in high-risk occupations such as farming and construction.
